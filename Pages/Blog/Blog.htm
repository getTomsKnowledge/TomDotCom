    Project Euler Problem #3: Largest Prime Factor

       The prime factors of 13195 are 5, 7, 13 and 29.
       What is the largest prime factor of the number 600851475143?


    This is a classic: it invites a brute-force approach but rewards insight into prime decomposition and optimization.

    Would you like to begin with your own implementation? Or shall I walk you through a scaffolded approach first, teasing out the mathematical and algorithmic core?


In working this excercise, I have made some (possibly-incomplete, possibly-incorrect) observations:

  • Our number is odd.
  • Our number is not a multiple of 5.
  • Our number is on the order of 6 x 1012 (i.e. pretty brutish)
    --> This is unequal-to-but-on-the-order-of 15 factorial, 2 to the 40th power, the 27th power of the natural number, the 63rd Fibonnacci Sequence term.
  • Every integer, n, in the set of integers greater than two contains a finite number of primes.
  • (An even number can't have prime factors other than 2: a prime-only factorization consists of two odd numbers.)
  • Factorization occurs about a 'center of factorization,' wherein a binary search can occur with quasi-mirrored steps between test numbers (one smaller than roughly n/4, the other larger)
  • Factoring of powers has a floor: 2. The maximum exponent for a factor of n is floor[log2(n)]. (As corollary, any other power factorization will be of a lesser degree/lower order.)
  • Is there a density or distribution function for primes?
  • logn(n) = 1.
  • logk(p) - logk(q) is finite for p,q < n, k ∈ ℕ.
  • logk(n) = Σlogk(pi)
  • n = Πpi
  • ∴ logk[Πpi] = Σlogk(pi)
  • Ruling out 2 in the two-factor solution space cuts the space in half. The solution space at each test factor in a sequential walk up from 2 probably follows a negative sigmoid curve?

So, then, we try some brute-force techniques:

  • / 2 = --> Nope.
  • / 3 = --> Nope, but has a patterned remainder?
  • / 7 = --> Nope.
  • / 11 = --> Nope, patterned remainder?
  • / 13 = --> Nope.

'K. What if we modulo, instead of a vanilla division? Will the remainder tell us something? Can we operate on modulos of different test factors, add them together, multiply, to extract some info?

Can we change basis to see if insights pop out: 2, 3, etc...? Can we perform 'spectral' analysis, look for signs of 'harmonics' and non-noise for numbers with increasingly-more factors (with a prime as noisiest)?
Can our number be generated by a well-known sequence?
